Designing a compact, mixed-signal robotics board requires balancing power demands, high-speed digital noise, and strict mechanical constraints. The **Desai Mochi** is built around the ESP32-C3 microcontroller, integrating audio, motion sensing, and dual power sources into a tight form factor.

This post serves as a comprehensive log of the hardware architecture, the specific component footprints selected, and the KiCad routing techniques used to pass a zero-error Design Rule Check (DRC) for factory assembly.

---

## 1. Power Architecture & Load Sharing

The Desai Mochi is designed to seamlessly switch between a 5V USB input and a 3.7V - 4.2V LiPo battery. This requires a robust load-sharing (power multiplexing) circuit to prevent the battery from back-feeding into the USB port or vice versa.

### The Schottky Diode (SS14)
* **Part Selection:** We utilized the **SS14 Schottky Diode** (JLCPCB Part: C2480).
* **Specifications:** Rated for **40V reverse voltage** and **1A continuous current**.
* **Footprint:** **SMA (DO-214AC)** (Dimensions: ~5.0mm x 2.5mm).
* **Design Rationale:** While an SS12 (20V) would suffice, the SS14 provides a wider safety margin. The Schottky diode is critical here because it features a very low forward voltage drop (0.3V - 0.5V), preserving battery life before the voltage hits the regulator.
* **Current Constraints:** The ESP32-C3 peaks at ~500mA during Wi-Fi transmission, and the onboard audio amplifier peaks near ~1A. It is crucial that the board's routing ensures the single 1A diode does not bottleneck the *entire* system load simultaneously.

## 2. Sensor Integration: The LSM6DS3 Accelerometer

Accelerometers measure physical forces by detecting microscopic changes in internal capacitance. Because of this, **electrical noise looks exactly like physical acceleration to the sensor.**

### Power Filtering (Decoupling)
* **The Error:** Initially, a 100µF bulk capacitor was placed on the accelerometer's power line.
* **The Correction:** Electrolytic/large capacitors have high Equivalent Series Resistance (ESR) and Inductance (ESL), making them too slow to respond to the high-frequency switching noise of the ESP32. We replaced this with a **0.1µF (100nF) Multi-Layer Ceramic Capacitor (MLCC)**.
* **Layout Rule:** This 0.1µF capacitor acts as a high-speed "local energy tank" and noise muffler. It was placed as close to the LSM6DS3's `VDD/VDDIO` pins as physically possible, with the ground side dropping directly into a `GND` via to keep the return loop infinitesimally short.

## 3. Interfaces and Connectors

The board features several peripheral connections routed to JST/Header connectors:
* **`J3_DIS1` (Display):** Carries the I2C lines (`SDA`, `SCL`), Power, and Ground. A critical mechanical clearance issue was resolved by moving a `GND` via away from the mechanical mounting pad (`Pad MP`) of this connector to prevent shorts with the screen's metal casing.
* **`J2_BAT1` (Battery):** Primary LiPo input.
* **`J5_SPEAKER1` (Audio):** Output for the onboard audio amplifier.
* **Tactile Switches:** We used standard 6x6mm SPST 1P1T tactile switches. The KiCad footprint `SW_Push_6mm` aligns perfectly with the standard JLCPCB assembly parts (6.5mm x 4.5mm pitch).

---

## 4. Mechanical Integration & 3D Printed Chassis

Because the Desai Mochi will be housed in a custom 3D-printed plastic chassis, mechanical mounting requires strict electrical isolation.

* **Mounting Holes:** Four **3.2mm M3 Non-Plated Through Holes (NPTH)** were placed in the corners of the board.
* **Ground Isolation:** The mounting holes were intentionally left "floating" (disconnected from the `GND` net). Tying mounting holes to ground in a plastic chassis offers no shielding benefits and introduces the risk of ground loops if conductive standoffs accidentally touch motor brackets or external sensors.
* **Keepout Zones:** A copper keepout zone (blocking both `F.Cu` and `B.Cu` layers) was implemented underneath the ESP32-C3 antenna to prevent the metal from detuning the RF signal.

## 5. PCB Layout & KiCad Polish

Before routing, several visual and manufacturing constraints were standardized:

* **Silkscreen Optimization:** Using KiCad's bulk-edit feature, all reference designators (`R1`, `C4`) and connector labels were standardized to a **Height of 0.8 mm**, **Width of 0.15 mm**, and **Thickness of 0.15 mm**. This ensures the factory ink printer does not output a blurry smudge. Text placed over exposed copper pads (Silk over Copper) was nudged into empty space to prevent missing label chunks.
* **F.Fab vs F.Silkscreen:** The Front Fabrication (`F.Fab`) layer contains component outlines and values for the assembly machines and technicians. It was left at its default scaling, as it is never printed on the physical board.

## 6. Overcoming DRC (Design Rule Check) Errors

The final phase involved clearing 37 initial DRC violations down to zero unconnected pads. Here are the specific layout challenges overcome:

### A. The 0.15mm Clearance Override
The LSM6DS3 footprint threw dozens of clearance errors because its pads are inherently 0.15mm apart. However, the global KiCad Net Class rule was defaulting to 0.2mm. By adjusting the Net Class clearance down to **0.15mm** in `Board Setup > Design Rules`, these false positives were cleared.

### B. Manufacturer Drill Limits
Several vias under chip `U2` were originally set to a 0.2mm drill size. JLCPCB standard manufacturing requires a minimum drill bit of 0.3mm. The vias were manually edited to a **0.3mm drill / 0.6mm diameter** to prevent drill bit snapping at the factory.

### C. Floating Ground Islands & Via Stitching
The copper pours on the top (`F.Cu`) and bottom (`B.Cu`) layers were physically isolated, causing a "Missing connection between items" error. This was resolved using **Via Stitching**—dropping `GND` vias in open spaces to act as copper bridges, tying the top and bottom layers into one massive, unified ground plane.

### D. Starved Thermals
The ground pours struggled to reach the `GND` pads of **U1, U2, and U6**. Signal traces were routed too closely, acting as walls and restricting the thermal relief to only one copper "spoke." A single spoke introduces a bottleneck for current and is highly susceptible to dissolving in the factory's acid etching bath. By nudging the adjacent signal traces away by just 0.1mm, the copper pour was able to flow in and create robust, multi-spoke thermal reliefs.
